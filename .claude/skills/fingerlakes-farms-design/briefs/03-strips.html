Component specs · Brief 3

Horizontal strips

Strips group products on /guide and /catalog — by category (Eggs, Cheese, Cream, Meat), by intent (Reorder last, In season this week), or by source (From Hartke's flock). The product card from Brief 1 is the strip's repeating unit. Today the strip hard-clips at the row edge — buyers don't know to swipe. This spec adds affordance without adding chrome.

Fade + peek

If content overflows, the rightmost card is intentionally peeked (60–70% visible) and a 64px white-to-transparent fade sits above the right edge. As the rail scrolls, the left fade appears symmetrically. No arrows on mobile — the peek does the work.

No affordance when none needed

If the rail fits within the strip, the fades and peek are suppressed. Cards left-align; the right edge is clean white. This is the only difference between Case 2 and a "broken" rail.

Desktop arrows + keyboard

≥1024px, two circular arrow buttons sit beside the strip header. Each scrolls by one card-stride. Arrow keys move focus card-by-card; the focused card auto-scrolls into the rail's center via scrollIntoView({block: "nearest", inline: "center"}).

Single-card category

One product = a featured horizontal composition: card on the left, producer/category editorial on the right. Solves the empty band by making the empty real estate do something — voice + a path to the rest of the category.

03

Single card · 1 product

e.g. "Cream" today · was a 1100px empty band

When the category holds one SKU, the strip flips to a featured composition: card on the left, editorial panel on the right. The empty real estate becomes voice — producer name, a one-sentence story, and a "Browse all dairy" link that surfaces the related supercategory. The visual rhythm of the page stays intact because the row height matches the other strips. On mobile the panel stacks below.

Spec v2 · all rails use scroll-snap-type: x proximity + scroll-snap-align: start per card. scroll-padding-inline: 24px keeps card-stride aligned to the strip's padding so arrow buttons land cleanly on the next card. Fade gradients are CSS pseudo-elements driven by a tiny scroll listener that toggles .is-scrolled and .is-end on the strip element. No JS framework needed. Tokens: colors_and_type.css.
